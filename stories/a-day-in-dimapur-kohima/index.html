Nobody tells you about Dimapur. Everyone flocks straight to Kohima.

I landed at Dimapur airport at 4pm on a Thursday in November, with one night to spare before catching the shared sumo to Kohima the next morning. Most travellers view Dimapur as a mere transit stop - a place to pass through, not stay in. Initially, I did too, until I discovered its charm.

The Hongkong Market in the evening was a revelation - loud, crowded, and selling everything from fresh pork to phone cases to Naga shawls in the same narrow lane. I stopped to eat at a small counter stall, where I had pork with bamboo shoot and rice for ₹80. The woman running the stall had been there since 6am and shared that the market comes alive at 4am, when the meat arrives from the village.

I stayed at a guesthouse near the railway station, which offered a clean room and attached bathroom for ₹600. The owner, an elderly Naga gentleman, advised me that the best time to visit Nagaland is not during the Hornbill Festival, as it has become too crowded. He recommended visiting in February, when the fields are yellow.

The shared sumo to Kohima departs from the main taxi stand at 6am, and I would suggest booking the front seat. The road winds its way up from 300 metres to 1500 metres in just two hours, so it's a good idea to carry a light jacket, even in November.

Nobody tells you about Dimapur, but you should consider spending a night there.
